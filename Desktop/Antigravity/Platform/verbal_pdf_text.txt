Although the committee initially regarded the proposal as overly cautious, subsequentdevelopments suggested that such restraint was not only justified but ______.

salutary
innovative
obligatory
perfunctory
obstructive

=== PAGE 2 ===
(difficulty: 4/5)

Explanation: Although sets up a contrast with “initially …overly cautious” so we know thatthe proposal must turn out to be something other than that. This could be “the right amountof caution” or “not cautious enough”. The second part of the sentence, “such restraint wasnot only justified but also _______” shows that our target needs to describe the “restraint”as something even better than / beyond “justified”
Salutary means producing good effects; beneficial. Not only reasonable but actuallybeneficial/ a good idea. Worth keeping. Innovative is a positive term that fits the correctgeneral idea of something positive, but it isn’t justified. It doesn’t pair well with “cautious”and “restraint”, and nothing in the passage indicates that it would be innovative. Not ouranswer. Obligatory means required; this is a very tempting choice. Keep it.Perfunctory (done with minimal effort) and obstructive (causing blockages) make no sense;in any case, these are both negative terms and we’re looking for something positive / evenbetter than justified to contrast the negative initial sentiment.
The best two answers are salutary and obligatory. But with obligatory, a closer look at thedefinition shows why it isn’t quite correct. If it just meant “necessary” or “required by thesituation” it could be our choice, but obligatory actually means something required by a legal,moral, or other rule. A closer synonym is compulsory. It doesn’t make sense to find out inthe end that such restraint is required by law; it makes more sense that such restraint turnedout to be a good idea / beneficial.

Psychological investigations into the "Foreign Language Effect" suggest that the linguistic mediumthrough which a moral dilemma is processed acts as a significant mediator of ethical output. Whenpresented with the "Trolley Problem"—a scenario requiring an active choice between allowing fivepeople to perish or intervening to sacrifice one—bilingual subjects are significantly more likely tofavor the utilitarian outcome when using their non-native tongue. This shift persists regardless ofwhether the subject has attained a high level of proficiency in the secondary language.Researchers, led by Albert Costa, hypothesize that the cognitive effort required to process anon-native language dampens the immediate, visceral response that typically supportsdeontological prohibitions against causing harm.

Singer, in his arguments for the universalmoral obligations that humans have to one another, observes that while most individuals wouldunquestionably rescue a drowning child in their immediate vicinity, a similar impulse becomes morereadily ignored when the victim is geographically remote, even if the sacrifice required (ruiningclothes vs. a marginal financial contribution to medical care) is identical.
